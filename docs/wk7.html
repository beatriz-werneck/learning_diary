6  Week 07: Classification I

6.1 Summary

This week is about classification, and how Earth Observation data can be analysed and classified under expert systems and machine learning lenses. To start with, expert system is a computer program designed to copy the way a human expert thinks to solve a problem. In this context, it works by using two main parts: knowledge base and inference engine. The knowledge base is a set of rules and/or logic provided by humans experts, and the inference engine is the part of the system that looks at the rules in the knowledge base to reach a conclusion. Here, it is important to notice that, as expert systems relies in human’s rules it is not imune to bias and errors. In addition, machine learning is when the computer learn from itself (without human’s coded-rules) by analysing the data. In other words, the system searches for patterns in the data (creating it’s own rules) to make conclusions, and this is called inductive learning.That said, the use of inductive learning to classify Earth Observation Data and identify land cover patterns have been used to analyse different contexts, such as urban expansion, air pollution, urban green spaces, forest fires, monitoring forests and illegal logging.

Some machine learning methods are: linear regression, classification and regression trees (CART), random forests and support vector machines (SVM). Linear regression fits a mathematical model to existing data so that it can be used to make predictions about new data. Decision trees split data into smaller and more similar groups, and there are two kinds of decision trees: classification and regression ones. Classification trees are used to sort data into discrete categories, such as deciding if a pixel in a satellite image is “forest” or “water”. And regression trees are used to predict continuous numeric values, for example the specific amount of air pollution in a certain area. Random forests are sets of many different decision trees working together. It is usually more accurate then single decision trees because, while a single decision tree might struggle with new data, a forest of trees provides a more robust and less overfitted classification through a “majority vote” system. Support Vector Machines (SVM) aim to find the best way to divide the data in two groups. Additionally, there are two main concepts behind SVM. The first one is the hyperplane, which means the “line” used to divide the data. And the second one is the margin, which means the buffer zone or empty space on either side of the line. Moreover, in SVM the margin should be as big as possible and it is a machine learning method that works well even if the model has a small number of training samples.

Despite their strengths, these methods have also limitations. Individual decision trees are highly susceptible to overfitting, where the model becomes so specific to the training pixels that it fails to accurately classify new, unseen data (Jensen, 2015). While Random Forests and SVMs are more robust, they are often criticised as “black boxes” because it can be difficult to see exactly how the computer reached its final conclusion (Sheykhmousa et al., 2020). Finally, no classification is 100% accurate because they are only as good as the information we give them. In other words, if the starting information has any degree of uncertainty, the final result will reflect that (GISGeography, 2014; Barsi et al., 2018). For example, atmospheric interference and shadows can distort spectral signatures, and even the human-provided training samples might contain errors that the computer then unintentionally replicates (Barsi et al., 2018).

6.2 Application

One example of imagery classification is the Hungarian government’s use of remote sensing to manage agricultural subsidies. In Hungary, the government uses a digital map system called the Land Parcel Identification System (LPIS) to check on farmers who ask for financial help. Instead of sending people to visit every farm, they use high-quality photos from planes and satellites to see exactly what is growing on the land. This approach is faster and more objective to ensure the right people get the correct amount of money (Barsi et al., 2018). The paper explains that this is done through visual interpretation, where experts look at high-quality aerial photos to draw lines around fields and identify crops. This is part of a larger government system called the Integrated Administration and Control System (IACS), which uses these images to check at least 5% of all farm claims every year to prevent mistakes or fraud (Barsi et al., 2018).

A second example is the study of Support Vector Machines (SVM) used to map different crops in places like Littleport, England, and La Mancha, Spain. Researchers discovered that this method is excellent at identifying specific crops like wheat, potatoes, and vineyards, even without a lot of starting data (Pal and Mather, 2005). To achieve this, the paper tested two specific ways for the computer to sort many different categories: “one-against-one” and “one-against-the-rest.” They found that the “one-against-one” method was both faster and more accurate. They also used a mathematical tool called a Radial Basis Kernel Function to help the computer find the best “lines” (hyperplanes) to separate complex data, such as different types of vegetation that look very similar from a distance (Pal and Mather, 2005).

When comparing these two papers, they show different but connected ways to use remote sensing. The work by Barsi et al. (2018) describes a massive administrative system used for national policy, where humans are still very involved in looking at the images to make legal decisions about money. In contrast, the study by Pal and Mather (2005) is about testing how automated algorithms like SVM can do the sorting work on their own. Essentially, while Barsi et al. (2018) focus on how a country uses the data to follow European Union rules, Pal and Mather (2005) use modern machine learning methods to support classification in remote sensing.

6.3 Reflection

This week, I gained a deeper understanding of how to transform raw satellite imagery into thematic maps through supervised and unsupervised classification techniques. Learning the practical workflow in Google Earth Engine, from masking clouds, using Sentinel-2 quality assurance (QA) bands, to training machine learning models like Random Forest and Support Vector Machines (SVM) showed me that classification might provide a balance between data-driven math and human expertise. I also realised that no classification is 100% perfect due to inherent data uncertainties. These skills will be valuable for my future work in urban planning and sustainable development, as they provide the evidence needed to monitor compliance with global agendas like the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). For example, I could use these methods to track urban expansion over decades or analyse the relationship between land-use changes and deforestation. Also, considering city-scale analysis, it is important to understand that Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA) is superior for high-resolution urban data, because it groups pixels into meaningful shapes like buildings or parks. In the future, I aim to integrate these Earth Observation workflows into city data pipelines to help planners make informed, sustainable decisions regarding green space management and urban growth.
